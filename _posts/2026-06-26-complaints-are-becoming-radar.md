Consumer complaints are becoming radar. Researchers now study angry emails, dispute forms, and call-center transcripts as early signals of trouble before the trouble becomes obvious.

For a long time, companies and regulators treated complaints as evidence after the fact. A customer had a bad experience, filed a complaint, and someone used that record to assign blame, refund money, or prove a pattern.

That old use still matters. But 2025 research points to a bigger shift: people are starting to use complaints as anticipatory infrastructure, meaning systems that help organizations see risk while it is still forming.

## The complaint arrives before the crisis

Complaints often contain weak signals. A customer may describe confusion about a fee, a delayed withdrawal, a sudden policy change, or a support team giving inconsistent answers. Each complaint may look small on its own, but patterns can tell analysts that something inside a company is starting to strain.

Researchers Zunza and Ndlovu show how natural language processing, a form of software that analyzes human language, can pull early-warning distress signals from complaint text. The interesting part is the source material. People do not need to fill out a formal risk report. They simply describe what happened to them, and their language may reveal pressure building inside the company.

Other scholars are finding that firms may time disclosures around moments that trigger complaints. That matters because complaints do more than reflect consumer frustration. They can also show how companies manage the timing of bad news.

## Regulators are listening differently

Regulators are also changing how they listen. SupTech, short for supervisory technology, helps regulators use data tools to monitor markets and institutions. In newer systems, complaint flows can become real-time inputs rather than dusty case files.

That creates a practical shift. A regulator who sees a sudden spike in a specific complaint type can investigate earlier. A firm that notices the same spike can fix a problem before customers organize, churn, or escalate.

This does not make complaints magical. People complain unevenly, and some groups have more time, trust, or access to complaint channels than others. Analysts still need context, judgment, and humility.

But the boundary is moving. Complaints used to live mostly in the world of accountability. Now researchers, firms, and regulators are pulling them into the world of anticipation.

That matters because the first public sign of a problem often comes from the people closest to it. When customers say something feels wrong, they may be doing more than venting. They may be describing the tremor before the quake.
